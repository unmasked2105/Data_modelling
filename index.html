What is
Data Modelling?

The art of deciding how your data is organised, structured, and stored — before a single byte is written.

🏗️

Think of it like an architect's blueprint. Before building a house, you decide where every room goes, how they connect, and what each space is used for. Data modelling does the same — for your data.

💡 Why It Matters

The impact of good
data modelling

Without a well-designed data model, even the best software falls apart. Here's what's at stake.

🚨

The problem without it

Data becomes duplicated, inconsistent, and fragmented across systems. Teams spend 80% of their time cleaning data instead of using it. Reports contradict each other. Decisions are made on bad information.

Problem
⚡

Faster, smarter queries

A well-modelled dataset can answer business questions in milliseconds instead of minutes. Analysts stop waiting. Dashboards load instantly. Decisions are made at the speed of thought.

Performance
🏢

One language for everyone

Data models create a shared vocabulary between engineers, analysts, and business teams. "Customer" means the same thing everywhere. No more "which customer table do you mean?"

Alignment
📈

Scales with your business

A bad data model that works at 100 users breaks at 10 million. Good models are designed to handle growth — in data volume, team size, and analytical complexity — without needing to be rebuilt from scratch.

Scalability
🔒

Data quality & trust

Models enforce rules. A "date of birth" field can't contain a future date. An "order" must have a "customer". These constraints prevent garbage from entering your system in the first place.

Quality
🌍

Used everywhere that matters

Netflix models what you watch to power recommendations. Banks model transactions to detect fraud. Hospitals model patient data to improve care. Data modelling is the silent backbone of every data-driven product.

Two types of workloads

Your data model must match how the data is used:

OLTP — Transactional OLAP — Analytical
Who uses it? End users, web apps Analysts, BI tools
What does it do? Fast reads/writes for daily operations Aggregates over millions of records
Example "Place an order", "Update a profile" "Revenue by region last quarter"
Data size GB to TB TB to PB
Storage style Row-oriented (all columns per row) Column-oriented (Parquet, etc.)
Model type ER / Normalised (3NF) Star Schema / Dimensional

🌍 In the Wild

Real-world examples

Data modelling isn't academic — it powers products you use every day.

Featured Example

How Netflix models your viewing habits

Netflix uses a dimensional model: a fact table of every viewing event (what you watched, when, for how long), surrounded by dimension tables for content, user, time, and device. This lets analysts instantly answer: "What do 25–34 year olds watch on Friday nights?"

📺
Dimensional model
01

Banking & Fraud Detection

Every transaction is modelled as a fact. The model enables real-time comparison against historical patterns. When a payment looks unusual, the model flags it in milliseconds — before the merchant even responds.

02

E-Commerce Recommendations

Amazon's "Customers also bought" is powered by a graph data model where products and users are nodes, and purchases are edges. The model discovers hidden relationships between products no human could find manually.

03

Healthcare & Patient Records

Hospitals use a carefully normalised ER model to link patient identity, diagnoses, treatments, and outcomes — while preventing duplication that could cause dangerous medication errors.

04

Social Media Feeds

Facebook and Twitter use graph models to represent connections between billions of users. Your feed is generated by traversing this model to find what your network engaged with most recently.
